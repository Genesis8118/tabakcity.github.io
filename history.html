Origin of "Tabaco": A Tale of Love, Miscommunication, and Legacy

This enchanting legend reveals the intriguing origins of Tabaco's name, stemming from a humorous misunderstanding between Spanish conquistadores and a protective father. As the story goes, the exclamation "Tabak ko! Tabak ko!"(My Bolo! My Bolo!) was misinterpreted as a joyous welcome by the clueless Spaniards, ultimately naming the city. Another version adds a romantic twist, describing a pre-Hispanic Tabaco named Pagkamoot (Love), led by the warrior Datu Maisog (Brave). The tales, whether funny or heroic, reflect the dual nature of Tabaquenos, embodying both warmth and passion. Congressman Edcel C. Lagman emphasizes that Tabaco is the home of a proud and gallant race, where the name, like the iconic bolo, carries a balance of beauty and strength.

History

Resilience and Renewal The rich history of Tabaco City traces back to 1587 when Franciscan missionaries initiated the conversion of Cagsawa's inhabitants to Catholicism. In 1616, Rev. Fr. Pedro de Alcareso became Tabaco's first permanent minister, constructing a stone church dedicated to St. John the Baptist, the city's patron saint. Tabaco evolved into a pivotal settlement, leading to the division of Albay into Partido de Tabaco and Partido de Iraya in the mid-17th century. Despite its charm, Tabaco faced adversities. In 1811, the town endured the devastating Bagiong Oguis, a powerful typhoon. In 1814, the eruption of Mayon Volcano claimed lives and disrupted livelihoods. Tabaco, though spared from the volcano's full wrath, took a decade to recover. In 1900, the Americans arrived under Col. Walter Howe, conquering Tabaco and nearby areas. Post-World War II, Tabaco witnessed a resilient community rebuilding their lives. As the Philippines gained independence, Tabaco emerged once again as a thriving town, a testament to its enduring spirit and the indomitable will of its people.
